Under both the Rashidun and Umayyad caliphates, architecture became a visual symbol of Islamic power, spirituality, and civilization. Mosques, palaces, fortresses, and caravanserais emerged across Arabia, the Levant, Persia, and North Africa. The earliest mosques, like the Prophet‚Äôs Mosque in Medina, were simple‚Äîbuilt of mud bricks with palm roofs. But this changed quickly. Caliph Umar expanded the mosque to accommodate growing congregations. The concept of the mihrab (niche indicating qibla) and minbar (pulpit) became standardized. Urban planning around the mosque became a norm in newly founded cities like Fustat and Kufa. The Umayyads made monumental contributions. The Dome of the Rock in Jerusalem (691 CE), commissioned by Caliph Abd al-Malik, was the first major Islamic architectural achievement. It combined Byzantine artistry with Qur'anic inscriptions and Islamic geometry. Another marvel was the Great Mosque of Damascus, featuring marble mosaics, massive arches, and one of the earliest minarets. Architectural features like horseshoe arches, courtyards, domes, fountains for ablution, and geometric tile work began to define Islamic aesthetics. Arabic calligraphy‚Äîespecially Qur'anic verses‚Äîbecame a decorative and spiritual design element. Materials ranged from marble and stone to stucco and wood, depending on local availability. Beyond mosques, administrative buildings, schools (madrasahs), and bathhouses (hammams) were built. Fortresses and city walls also reflected Islamic architectural planning, as in the cities of Baghdad and Aleppo. Roads and caravanserais connected regions, boosting trade and movement. Thus, the Caliphate Era was not just a religious and military peak‚Äîit laid the foundation of Islamic art and architecture, reflecting unity, transcendence, and cultural richness.
